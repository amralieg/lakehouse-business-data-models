The EU regulatory shape is meaningfully different from the US:
      The EU Drinking Water Directive sets PFAS limits at 0.5 µg/L for "PFAS total" and 0.1 µg/L for
      "sum of 20 priority compounds." That's a fundamentally different reporting structure than the US
      per-compound MCLs + hazard index.
      REACH restriction proposals (Feb 2023 by 5 member states) target the broader PFAS class, not
      individual compounds.
      The Water Framework Directive defines surface-water status differently from US frameworks.
Suggestion (for the team to validate): rather than re-citing every reference, consider adding a
 jurisdiction or regulatory_region dimension to compliance.regulatory_requirement and
 compliance.regulatory_agency , with parallel reference paths for US / EU / other regions. Tables like
 pfas_monitoring , compliance_permit , ccr_period , dmr , mor would then carry both
jurisdictional flavors.

Finding 3 - quality.pfas_monitoring is the most visible empty
stub
The dedicated PFAS table has two columns: pfas_monitoring_id (PK) and sampling_schedule_id
(FK). The description claims:
  "Captures individual PFAS compound results (PFOA, PFOS, PFNA, PFHxS, PFBS, HFPO-DA/GenX),
  hazard index calculation for PFNA+PFHxS+PFBS+HFPO-DA, MCL compliance status, monitoring period,
  sampling point, and treatment response trigger status."
None of those concepts are present in the actual table - there's nowhere to store a PFAS measurement.
Two-part suggestion (for the team to validate):
   1. Decide whether pfas_monitoring should exist at all - quality.analytical_result (42
      cols, well-built) already has contaminant_id , mcl_value , mclg_value , detection_limit ,
       result_value , unit_of_measure . PFAS data has a natural home there. The dedicated table
      may be redundant.
   2. If it stays, add a PFAS compound master - both US (per-compound MCLs + hazard index) and
      EU (sum-of-20 + class restriction) regulatory frameworks require compound-level granularity. Today
      the contaminant table could carry these, but PFAS compounds aren't enumerated anywhere.
For a deeper look at the regulatory and detection landscape - including 76 references covering EU Directive
2020/2184, the European Food Safety Authority TWI, and treatment effectiveness per compound - the
reference paper behind [INTERNAL-DEMO-PLATFORM] is downloadable at [INTERNAL-DEMO-DOMAIN].

Smaller observations
Worth flagging but lower priority than the three above:
     treatment.facility has no latitude / longitude - surprising for the most map-critical
    entity. Meanwhile online_instrument , source_water_intake , and water_sample all have
    GIS columns. Inconsistent.
    No ML / AI prediction storage anywhere in 377 tables. For a Data & AI conference release, this is
    the most surprising gap. Modern water utilities run predictive models (PFAS breakthrough, demand
    forecasting, leak detection). A generic prediction_event entity with predicted_value ,
     actual_value , confidence , model_version would serve many domains.
    No customer-impact linkage on incidents. main_break , network_isolation_event ,
     overflow_event capture the asset-side fact, but no entity links them to affected customers
    ( population_at_risk , affected_zones , expected_restoration_at ,
     public_notification_id ).
    MVM excludes Laboratory but includes Finance. The entire laboratory domain
    (chain_of_custody, sample_collection_event, test_result, lab_instrument, qc_batch) is ECM-only.
    Meanwhile finance (debt_instrument, GL, journal_entry) is in MVM. For a small-to-mid utility
    starting fresh, drinking-water compliance reporting depends on lab data; corporate treasury is
    usually outsourced or in a separate ERP.
     process_control.scada_tag is ECM-only. SCADA is central to treatment and distribution
    operations at any scale; would belong in MVM with at least a basic tag taxonomy.
     metric_views field is empty across all 15 domains. The structure is in place but unpopulated.
    Shipping 50-100 pre-baked KPIs alongside the schema (water loss %, non-revenue-water %,
    energy intensity kWh/ML, biosolids beneficial-reuse %, CCR exceedance rate) would be a strong
    "Day 1 dashboard" narrative for [CONFERENCE], and many are derivable from existing tables without schema
    changes.
    MVM/ECM "same attribute depth" claim is inconsistent. Several backbone tables are richer in
    MVM than ECM ( facility 5 vs 2 cols, compliance_violation 7 vs 3, overflow_event 8 vs
    3, network_reading 7 vs 4). Either the README claim or the ECM needs reconciliation.

A broader observation - ontology-driven generation
Several of the gaps above (the empty pfas_monitoring table, the missing PFAS compound master, the
absent risk-pathway and receptor concepts) might be addressable in a single step by introducing an
ontology-first stage to the generation pipeline. As a separate experiment, I ran the [INTERNAL-DEMO-PLATFORM] reference
paper through OntoBricks - a Databricks Labs project (maintainer: Benoit Cayla) that produces an OWL
ontology from a source document.
The output captures PFAS-specific entities (long-chain vs short-chain PFAS, PFAS compound master),
treatment technologies (GAC, ion exchange, multimedia sand filter), exposure-pathway and receptor
concepts (human / ecological), risk-assessment relationships, and a regulatory-standard layer - most of
which are not yet represented in the relational model:




If the vibe-modelling-agent could accept an ontology as a seed for relational generation (or use one as a
post-generation validator), gaps like an empty pfas_monitoring table would be hard to produce - the
ontology would demand the entities exist, and validation could flag any class missing concrete attributes.
Worth a conversation with Benoit and Amr - there might be a complementary pipeline shape between
OntoBricks (semantic layer) and the vibe-modelling-agent (relational layer) that strengthens both for the
[CONFERENCE] release and beyond.

Closing note
The model has real bones. The [CONFERENCE] release framing - "industry data models you can use for free" - is
achievable, and the well-built tables already demonstrate what good looks like. The biggest risks before
launch are the stubs in central entities (which a customer will hit on first query) and the US-only regulatory
scope (which limits applicability outside North America).
Again - and worth repeating because it matters - I am not a water utilities domain expert. [INTERNAL-DEMO-PLATFORM] was a
fictional exercise to imagine what a real European utility platform might look like. The findings here should
be treated as observations from someone who read carefully, and validated by a practitioner before any are
acted on.

point.
