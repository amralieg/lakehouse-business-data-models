1. Executive Summary
The automotive data model repository provides an impressively comprehensive and well-structured Lakehouse data model for the automotive manufacturing industry. It is available in two scope variations - a Minimum Viable Model (MVM, 14 domains, 209 tables) and an Expanded Coverage Model (ECM, 19 domains, 548 tables). Both models are designed for deployment on the Databricks platform and include DDL schemas, metric views, sample data, DBML diagrams, and RDF ontologies.
This review evaluates whether the model adequately covers the main parts of the automotive value chain: R&D, Purchasing, Production, Logistics, Sales, Aftersales, and Field Services - and whether the relevant operational systems are considered.
---
2. Value Chain Coverage Assessment
2.1 R&D (Research & Development) - COVERED
Model Domains: Engineering, Vehicle (product_catalog)
Strengths:
The Engineering domain is well-represented with subdomains for design engineering, component validation, program governance, and supply planning.
Key R&D artifacts are modeled: engineering BOMs, design specifications, CAD model references, DVP (Design Verification Plan) plans, FMEA records, ECU specifications, powertrain specs, material specifications.
Engineering Change Management (ECO/ECN) is properly included.
Vehicle program management with milestone tracking (P0 through SOP) supports APQP phase-gate processes.
Prototype build and validation test records are present in the ECM.
Digital twin references are included (ECM only).
Operational Systems Referenced: Siemens Teamcenter, CATIA, ENOVIA, PTC Windchill - these are the correct PLM/CAD systems for the automotive industry.
Gaps / Suggestions:
No explicit domain for advanced R&D topics such as simulation data management (CFD, FEA result datasets), test bench data, or autonomous driving development data (sensor datasets, scenario libraries). While CFD/FEA/NVH are mentioned in descriptions, dedicated data products for simulation results would strengthen this area.
Patent and IP management data is absent - not critical for a Lakehouse model, but worth noting for completeness.
Requirements management (e.g., DOORS/Polarion/Jira integration) is mentioned implicitly through "linked requirements" but lacks a dedicated requirements traceability data product.
Verdict: Well covered. The model captures the essential R&D data structures. The ECM variant is particularly strong here.
---
2.2 Purchasing / Procurement - COVERED
Model Domains: Supply, Procurement (ECM only)
Strengths:
The Supply domain covers procurement operations, supplier management, quality assurance (PPAP), and inbound logistics even in the MVM.
The ECM adds a full Procurement domain with contract governance, procurement operations (PO, requisitions, approvals, sourcing events), spend analytics, and supplier management.
Supplier evaluation, scorecards, compliance, and development plans are well represented.
RFQ/RFQ response workflows are modeled.
Scheduling agreements and delivery schedules reflect JIT/JIS practices common in automotive.
Operational Systems Referenced: SAP MM/SRM concepts are clearly reflected in the data structures (purchase requisitions, purchase orders, scheduling agreements, info records, goods receipts).
Gaps / Suggestions:
The MVM excludes the entire Procurement domain (marked as "Domain not in MVM"). For small-to-mid automotive businesses, basic purchase order and supplier management should arguably be in the MVM. The current workaround of having "stub" tables (purchaseorder, poline, supplier) in the MVM Supply domain partially addresses this, but these are marked as "MVM only (stub or new)" which feels incomplete.
No explicit modeling of e-procurement portals or supplier collaboration platforms (e.g., Ariba, Covisint/SupplyOn).
Commodity management and hedging (important for raw material purchasing in automotive) is absent.
Verdict: Well covered in the ECM; adequate but thin in the MVM.
---
2.3 Production / Manufacturing - COVERED
Model Domains: Manufacturing, Quality, Inventory
Strengths:
The Manufacturing domain is comprehensive with plant operations, production execution, quality assurance, and supply management subdomains.
Key manufacturing concepts are modeled: production orders, build sequences, build stages, vehicle builds, production confirmations, material consumption, downtime events, shop floor events, WIP inventory.
The Quality domain includes the full IATF 16949 / APQP toolkit: FMEA, control plans, inspection plans, SPC charts, PPAP submissions, defect management, and audit capabilities.
Manufacturing BOM (mBOM) and routing are properly separated from the engineering BOM.
Capacity planning, production scheduling, shift management, and work center modeling are present.
Operational Systems Referenced: SAP PP (Production Planning), MES (Manufacturing Execution System) concepts are well reflected. References to AGV (Automated Guided Vehicle) routes and movements show awareness of Industry 4.0 shopfloor automation.
Gaps / Suggestions:
No explicit MES integration layer - while shopfloorevent captures MES-like data, a more structured MES data model (work instructions, operator assignments per station, cycle time tracking) would be beneficial.
OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness) is implicitly calculable from downtimeevent and productionconfirmation data, but an explicit OEE metric view or pre-aggregated OEE data product would be a practical addition.
Paint shop, body shop, and assembly-specific process parameters could be modeled more explicitly (currently generalized under process_parameter in the ECM).
Energy consumption tracking per production line/plant is missing - increasingly important for ESG reporting.
Verdict: Strong coverage. Manufacturing is one of the best-modeled areas.
---
2.4 Logistics - COVERED
Model Domains: Logistics, Inventory
Strengths:
The Logistics domain is well-structured with carrier management, compound operations, customs documentation, and transport planning subdomains.
Finished vehicle logistics are specifically modeled: vehicle compounds, compound movements, vehicle handovers, vehicle transport orders, vessel voyages, load plans.
Inbound logistics (CKD/SKD kits, inbound shipments/inspections) are covered under the Supply domain.
Multi-modal transport is addressed: shipping lines, rail car assignments, vessel voyages, and road transport.
Port processing and customs (import/export declarations) are present in the ECM.
In-transit inventory tracking bridges logistics and inventory management.
Operational Systems Referenced: TMS (Transport Management System) concepts are well reflected. The model structure aligns with SAP TM patterns.
Gaps / Suggestions:
Yard management (vehicle compound storage optimization, slot assignments) is partially covered by vehicle_compound but could be more detailed.
Last-mile delivery (dealer delivery scheduling, customer home delivery for direct-to-consumer models) is underrepresented.
Returnable packaging / container management (Ladungstraeger-Management) - a critical operational topic in automotive logistics - is not explicitly modeled.
Dangerous goods handling is not addressed.
Verdict: Well covered, particularly for finished vehicle distribution. Inbound parts logistics could be slightly more detailed.
---
2.5 Sales - COVERED
Model Domains: Sales, Dealer, Customer
Strengths:
The Sales domain covers lead management, quote/order processing, fleet contracts, incentive programs, and sales territory management.
The Dealer domain is notably strong with dealer operations, inventory management, sales performance, and service support - reflecting the franchise dealer model central to automotive.
Customer domain includes customer identity, engagement analytics, loyalty management, and vehicle ownership.
Fleet sales are well-modeled with fleet contracts, fleet contract lines, and fleet accounts.
Incentive programs and MSRP scheduling are properly included.
The configurator flow (quote to order to vehicle order to production) is traceable through the model.
Operational Systems Referenced: CRM concepts (leads, opportunities, campaigns, activities) align with Salesforce/SAP CRM patterns. DMS (Dealer Management System) integration is referenced.
Gaps / Suggestions:
Online/digital sales channels (configurator sessions, online orders, e-commerce) are absent - increasingly important as OEMs move to agency/direct models.
Used vehicle / pre-owned sales are only covered via usedvehicleappraisal in the dealer domain (ECM only). A more complete CPO (Certified Pre-Owned) workflow would be valuable.
Financing and leasing (F&I - Finance & Insurance) is not modeled, despite being a major revenue stream. This is a notable gap.
Subscription-based vehicle models (vehicle-as-a-service) are partially covered under the Mobility domain but not connected to the Sales flow.
Verdict: Good coverage of traditional OEM-to-dealer sales. Gaps in digital sales, F&I, and subscription models.
---
2.6 Aftersales - COVERED
Model Domains: Aftersales, Dealer (service_support)
Strengths:
The Aftersales domain is one of the most detailed in the model, with subdomains for dealer management, parts logistics, product catalog, service operations, and technical support.
Warranty management is comprehensive: warranty policies, warranty claims, goodwill adjustments, warranty parts returns, service contracts, and service contract claims.
Recall management: recall campaigns, campaign VINs, recall completions, and service campaigns.
Technical Service Bulletins (TSBs) and repair operations are well-modeled.
Parts distribution with service parts, parts order lines, and parts returns supports the aftermarket supply chain.
Labor time standards and MSRP price books support workshop costing.
Operational Systems Referenced: SAP CS (Customer Service), DMS integration concepts are present. The model reflects standard warranty systems (e.g., OEM warranty platforms).
Gaps / Suggestions:
Connected vehicle diagnostics integration (OBD-II live data feeding into aftersales workflows) is only present in the Mobility domain (ECM), not directly linked to aftersales service operations.
Over-the-air (OTA) software updates as an aftersales activity (vs. recall alternative) could be better connected to the aftersales service flow.
Customer self-service portals (appointment booking, service history access) are not modeled.
Extended warranty / third-party warranty provider data is limited.
Verdict: Strong coverage. Aftersales is one of the model's highlights.
---
2.7 Field Services - PARTIALLY COVERED
Model Domains: No dedicated domain; partially distributed across Aftersales, Dealer, and Mobility
Assessment:
Field services in the automotive context can mean different things:
a) Dealer Field Representatives (OEM to Dealer support):
dealerrepassignment exists in the Sales domain (ECM only).
Dealer quality assessments and performance scorecards exist.
However, a structured field visit / field activity tracking model for OEM field representatives is absent.
b) Mobile Service / Roadside Assistance:
Not modeled at all. No data products for roadside assistance cases, mobile mechanic dispatching, towing events, or breakdown management.
c) Field Quality & Warranty [INTERNAL-TEAM]:
qualityppapsubmission, supplierqualityevent, and field_return exist in the Quality domain.
warrantyqualitysignal is in the ECM.
But a dedicated "field quality investigation" or "field failure analysis" data product is missing.
d) Connected Vehicle Field Data:
The Mobility domain (ECM only) has telemetry events, DTC events, predictive maintenance alerts, and remote diagnostics - these are strong building blocks for field service intelligence.
However, this domain is entirely excluded from the MVM.
Gaps / Suggestions:
A dedicated Field Services domain (or subdomain) would strengthen the model - covering field technician dispatching, mobile service orders, roadside assistance, field quality investigations, and field engineering reports.
Integration between connected vehicle telemetry and service scheduling (proactive maintenance triggers) should be modeled as a cross-domain workflow.
Verdict: Partially covered. Field services concepts are scattered across multiple domains without a cohesive structure. This is the weakest area of the value chain coverage.

4. Cross-Cutting Observations
MVM vs. ECM balance: The MVM excludes 5 entire domains (Asset, Mobility, Procurement, Product, Workforce). While the MVM is designed to be lean, excluding Procurement and Mobility entirely may be too aggressive - basic purchasing and connected vehicle data are increasingly core even for smaller operations.
Industry 4.0 / Digitalization: The model has some Industry 4.0 elements (AGV, digital twins, OTA) but could be more explicit about IoT sensor data, edge computing data flows, and AI/ML model metadata (e.g., predictive quality models, computer vision inspection results).
ESG / Sustainability: Environmental, Social, and Governance data is underrepresented. CO2 emissions tracking, energy consumption, water usage, social compliance audits, and ESG reporting structures are increasingly mandatory (CSRD, EU Taxonomy) and not modeled.
Data Governance: The model includes compliance and audit domains but does not explicitly model data catalog metadata, data quality rules, data lineage, or data ownership - which are important for a Lakehouse architecture.
Metric Views: Both models include metric views (101 in ECM, 128 in MVM), which is a practical addition for analytics readiness. The MVM actually has more metric views than the ECM, suggesting a stronger analytics focus in the lighter model.
---
5. Overall Assessment

Overall Verdict: The automotive data model is a well-structured, comprehensive reference model that covers the majority of the automotive value chain. The ECM variant in particular offers enterprise-grade breadth with 19 domains and 548 tables. The main area for improvement is Field Services, which should be consolidated into a dedicated domain. Secondary recommendations include adding F&I (Finance & Insurance) for sales, strengthening the MVM's procurement coverage, and adding ESG/sustainability data products.
